# Cerebral_Microbleeds (Small Chronic Brain Hemorrhages)

* **Full Name / Aliases:** Cerebral Microbleeds (CMBs), Microbleeds, Cerebral Microhemorrhages, Chronic Microhemorrhages
* **ICD-10 Code:** 
  * I67.89 (Other specified cerebrovascular diseases - commonly used as there is no unique, specific ICD-10 code for microbleeds)
  * I68.8 (Other cerebrovascular disorders in diseases classified elsewhere - e.g., cerebral amyloid angiopathy causing microbleeds)
* **Affected Organ / System:** Central Nervous System (CNS) / Cerebral Microvasculature (arterioles, capillaries, and venules)

---

## 1. Clinical Presentation & Symptoms
* **Common Presenting Symptoms:**
  * **Asymptomatic (Majority):** CMBs are subclinical, tiny focal hemorrhages that do not cause acute focal deficits. They are almost always discovered incidentally on highly sensitive MRI sequences.
  * **Cognitive Impairment & Dementia:** A high burden of CMBs is strongly associated with executive dysfunction, psychomotor slowing, and progressive cognitive decline (vascular or mixed dementia).
  * **Transient Neurological Deficits ("Amyloid Spells"):** Transient, recurrent sensory or motor symptoms lasting minutes (often mimicking TIAs or migraines) can occur in patients with cerebral amyloid angiopathy-associated CMBs due to localized cortical irritation.
  * **Gait Disturbance:** Associated with diffuse hypertensive microangiopathy and white matter disease.
* **Clinical Significance:** CMBs serve as a critical imaging marker for severe underlying microvascular disease, representing a significantly elevated risk for future major symptomatic intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) and ischemic stroke.

## 2. Radiographic & Imaging Findings
### Spatial & Morphological Patterns
* **Size:** Small, round, or ovoid foci of signal void, typically defined as **2 to 5 mm** (up to 10 mm) in diameter.
* **Distribution Patterns (Critical for Etiology):**
  * **Lobar Distribution (Cortical-Subcortical Junction):** Microbleeds are confined to the cortex and subcortical white matter, sparing the deep gray matter and brainstem. (Highly specific for **Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy [CAA]**, typically in elderly patients).
  * **Deep & Infratentorial Distribution:** Microbleeds are located in the basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, brainstem, and cerebellum. (Highly specific for **Hypertensive Microangiopathy / Arteriolosclerosis**).
  * **Mixed Distribution:** Microbleeds are found in both lobar and deep structures, typically representing long-standing, severe hypertensive angiopathy, or a combination of hypertension and CAA.

### Signal & Density Characteristics
* **MRI Signal Profile (The only reliable imaging modality):**
  * **T2* Gradient Recalled Echo (GRE) & Susceptibility-Weighted Imaging (SWI):** The diagnostic hallmark is **focal, homogeneous, round "blooming" artifacts** (areas of signal loss/black spots). SWI is significantly more sensitive than GRE, showing more microbleeds and making them appear larger than their actual histopathological size.
  * **T1- and T2-weighted Spin-Echo:** Typically invisible (iso- to hypointense, but too small to resolve).
  * **FLAIR:** Normal. Unlike cavernomas, microbleeds do not show surrounding FLAIR hyperintensity (edema or gliosis) because they are chronic and subclinical.
* **CT Density Profile:**
  * **Non-contrast head CT:** **Invisible**. Standard CT cannot resolve microbleeds due to their tiny size and lack of susceptibility-weighting.

## 3. Pathognomonic & Key Diagnostic Features
* **Pathognomonic Signs:**
  * **Focal Blooming Artifacts on SWI/T2\*:** Small, round, punctate black spots in the parenchyma.
* **Key Radiologic Clues:**
  * **Boston Criteria v2.0 (for CAA diagnosis):** Updated in 2022 to improve diagnostic sensitivity in patients aged $\ge 50$ presenting with spontaneous ICH, cognitive decline, or transient focal neurological episodes. 
    * **Probable CAA** can be diagnosed on MRI if the patient has:
      * **At least two** strictly lobar hemorrhagic lesions (any combination of lobar microbleeds, lobar ICH, or cortical superficial siderosis [cSS]); **OR**
      * **At least one** strictly lobar hemorrhagic lesion **plus** at least one characteristic non-hemorrhagic white matter feature:
        * Severe perivascular spaces in the centrum semiovale (CS-PVS).
        * Multispot white matter hyperintensities (MS-WMH).
    * **Exclusion Criteria:** Must show no deep hemorrhagic lesions (in basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, brainstem, or deep cerebellum) on SWI/GRE and have no other explanation for hemorrhage.

## 4. Disease Progression & Temporal Evolution
* **Short-Term Progression:** Individual microbleeds do not evolve or grow. Once blood leaks, red blood cells break down, and the hemosiderin is permanently ingested by perivascular macrophages. This hemosiderin remains in the tissue, acting as a permanent "magnetic scar" that is visible on SWI indefinitely.
* **Long-Term Evolution:** 
  * Rather than individual lesions changing, the *number* of microbleeds accumulates over time as the underlying microangiopathy progresses.
  * Patients with $\ge 5$ or especially $\ge 10$ microbleeds have a high cumulative risk of sustaining a major, life-threatening lobar or deep intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH), particularly if they are treated with anticoagulation, thrombolysis, or antiplatelet therapies.

## 5. Management, Treatment & Follow-up Protocol
* **Standard Treatment Options:**
  * **No Direct Treatment:** There is no therapy to remove or treat existing microbleeds. Treatment is entirely directed at managing the underlying microangiopathy.
  * **Hypertension Management:** Strict blood pressure control (e.g., target SBP <130 mmHg) is the most effective intervention to prevent the formation of new microbleeds and reduce the risk of future major ICH.
  * **Antithrombotic Decisions (Clinical Dilemma):**
    * The presence of microbleeds is a key factor when weighing the risks and benefits of initiating or continuing anticoagulants (e.g., for atrial fibrillation) or antiplatelets.
    * If a patient has a high microbleed count ($\ge 10$ lobar microbleeds, strongly suggesting severe CAA), the risk of causing a fatal ICH may outweigh the ischemic prevention benefits of anticoagulation, prompting consideration of alternative strategies (e.g., Left Atrial Appendage Occlusion [LAAO]).
  * **Avoidance of Thrombolysis:** In acute ischemic stroke, a microbleed count $>10$ on pre-treatment MRI is associated with a high risk of symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage post-tPA, representing a relative contraindication in clinical guidelines.
* **Follow-up Imaging Guidelines:**
  * **Surveillance MRI:** Contrast is not needed. Routine follow-up MRI with SWI or T2* GRE is performed every 1 to 2 years in patients with known cerebral amyloid angiopathy or severe hypertensive microangiopathy, particularly if they require long-term antithrombotic therapies.
