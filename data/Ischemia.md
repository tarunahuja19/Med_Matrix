# Ischemia (Ischemic Stroke / Cerebral Ischemia)

* **Full Name / Aliases:** Ischemic Stroke, Cerebral Ischemia, Cerebral Infarction, Acute Ischemic Stroke (AIS), Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA) (transient symptoms without permanent infarction)
* **ICD-10 Code:** 
  * I63.9 (Cerebral infarction, unspecified)
  * I63.3 (Cerebral infarction due to thrombosis of precerebral arteries)
  * I63.4 (Cerebral infarction due to embolism of precerebral arteries)
  * G45.9 (Transient cerebral ischemic attack, unspecified)
* **Affected Organ / System:** Central Nervous System (CNS) / Brain and the Cerebrovascular System

---

## 1. Clinical Presentation & Symptoms
* **Common Presenting Symptoms (FAST):** 
  * **Face Drooping:** Sudden asymmetry or numbness of the face.
  * **Arm Weakness:** Sudden weakness or numbness in one limb (usually unilateral hemiparesis).
  * **Speech Difficulty:** Sudden difficulty speaking (dysarthria) or understanding speech (aphasia).
  * **Other Symptoms:** Sudden visual loss (monocular or homonymous hemianopia), loss of balance/coordination (ataxia), vertigo, or sudden severe headache.
* **Clinical Course:** Hyperacute/Acute onset. Symptoms peak within minutes of arterial occlusion. In TIA, symptoms resolve completely within 24 hours (usually <1 hour) without imaging evidence of infarction.

## 2. Radiographic & Imaging Findings
### Spatial & Morphological Patterns
* **Vascular Territory Distribution:** Unlike tumors, ischemia strictly adheres to vascular territories (e.g., Middle Cerebral Artery [MCA], Anterior Cerebral Artery [ACA], Posterior Cerebral Artery [PCA], or vertebrobasilar system). 
* **Morphology:** Wedge-shaped, cortical-subcortical lesions extending to the cortical ribbon.
* **Lacunar Infarcts:** Small, round, or ovoid lesions (<15 mm) in deep penetrating arteries (basal ganglia, internal capsule, thalamus, pons).
* **Watershed (Borderzone) Infarcts:** Occur at the junctions between major arterial territories (e.g., MCA/ACA boundary), often due to systemic hypotension or severe carotid stenosis.

### Signal & Density Characteristics (Temporal Evolution)
* **Hyperacute Stage (<6 hours):**
  * **MRI (Gold Standard):** 
    * **DWI / ADC:** Demonstrates **restricted diffusion** within minutes of onset. The lesion is **hyperintense** (bright) on DWI and **hypointense** (dark) on ADC maps. This represents cytotoxic edema (cellular swelling restricting extracellular water movement).
    * **T2 / FLAIR:** Typically normal in the first few hours (DWI-FLAIR mismatch).
  * **CT:** Often normal or showing subtle signs: loss of gray-white matter differentiation (e.g., "insular ribbon sign"), obscuration of the lentiform nucleus, and sulcal effacement.
* **Acute Stage (6–24 hours):**
  * **MRI:** T2 and FLAIR signal intensity begins to increase (hyperintense). DWI remains bright; ADC remains dark.
  * **CT:** Increasingly visible hypodensity in the affected vascular territory with worsening swelling/sulcal effacement.
* **Subacute Stage (24 hours to 2 weeks):**
  * **MRI:** T2/FLAIR hyperintensity peaks. ADC values begin to rise and "pseudonormalize" (around day 7–10, appearing isointense) before becoming hyperintense. Cortical laminar necrosis may present as T1 hyperintensity along the cortex after 3–5 days.
  * **CT:** Well-demarcated hypodensity. Edema and mass effect peak at 3–5 days, posing a risk of herniation. Between days 7–14, contrast enhancement (gyral pattern) peaks due to blood-brain barrier breakdown, which can sometimes mask the hypodensity (the "fogging effect").
* **Chronic Stage (>2 weeks to months):**
  * **MRI:** DWI is normal or hypointense; ADC is hyperintense (bright, due to facilitated diffusion). The necrotic tissue liquefies, leaving a cavity with CSF signal (T1 dark, T2 bright, FLAIR dark) surrounded by a rim of glial scar tissue (FLAIR hyperintense gliosis).
  * **CT:** Well-defined CSF-density cavity (encephalomalacia) with associated focal volume loss and compensatory expansion of adjacent ventricles (ventriculomegaly ex vacuo).

## 3. Pathognomonic & Key Diagnostic Features
* **Pathognomonic Signs:**
  * **DWI-FLAIR Mismatch:** Positive DWI (bright) but negative FLAIR (no signal abnormality). This confirms hyperacute ischemia within the 4.5-hour thrombolysis window.
  * **Hyperdense MCA Sign:** On non-contrast CT, a hyperdense segment of the horizontal (M1) middle cerebral artery, representing an acute intravascular thrombus (highly specific, 100% positive predictive value).
* **Key Radiologic Clues:**
  * **Insular Ribbon Sign:** Hypodensity and loss of normal gray-white distinction of the insular cortex, one of the earliest CT signs of MCA infarction.

## 4. Disease Progression & Temporal Evolution
* **Short-Term Progression:** 
  * Progressive cytotoxic and vasogenic edema peaking at 72–96 hours, potentially causing midline shift, ventricular compression, or fatal brain herniation (especially in "Malignant MCA" infarction).
  * **Hemorrhagic Transformation:** Petechial hemorrhage or hematoma within the infarcted tissue (often occurring 24–48 hours post-ictus, exacerbated by reperfusion therapy).
* **Long-Term Evolution / Complications:**
  * Liquefactive necrosis leads to encephalomalacia (cavitation) and focal cerebral atrophy.
  * Wallerian degeneration: Degeneration of downstream white matter tracts (e.g., corticospinal tract in the brainstem following a massive cortical motor stroke).

## 5. Management, Treatment & Follow-up Protocol
* **Standard Treatment Options:**
  * **Acute Reperfusion:**
    * **Intravenous Thrombolysis:** Recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (rtPA, e.g., Alteplase) or Tenecteplase administered within 4.5 hours of symptom onset (once intracranial hemorrhage is ruled out).
    * **Endovascular Thrombectomy (EVT):** Mechanical retrieval of a large vessel occlusion (LVO) in the anterior circulation within 6 hours (and up to 24 hours based on perfusion imaging showing mismatch between core infarct and penumbra).
  * **Medical Management:** Antiplatelets (Aspirin, Clopidogrel), statin therapy, tight blood pressure and glycemic control, and anticoagulation for cardioembolic stroke (e.g., atrial fibrillation).
  * **Decompressive Hemicraniectomy:** For malignant cerebral edema with mass effect in patients <60 years old.
* **Follow-up Imaging Guidelines:**
  * **Post-Thrombolysis:** Non-contrast head CT or MRI at 24 hours post-infusion to rule out hemorrhagic transformation prior to initiating antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapies.
  * **Secondary Prevention Workup:** Carotid duplex ultrasound or CT/MR Angiography (CTA/MRA) of the head and neck to evaluate for carotid stenosis or arterial dissection; Echocardiogram to evaluate for a cardioembolic source.
