# Cerebral_Cyst (Fluid-Filled Brain Cysts)

* **Full Name / Aliases:** Cerebral Cyst, Intracranial Cyst, Arachnoid Cyst, Epidermoid Cyst (Epidermoid Brain Tumor), Colloid Cyst, Porencephalic Cyst, Pineal Cyst
* **ICD-10 Code:** 
  * G93.0 (Cerebral cysts)
  * Q04.6 (Congenital cerebral cysts - e.g., congenital porencephaly or congenital arachnoid cyst)
  * D33.0 (Benign neoplasm of brain, supratentorial - often used for epidermoid or colloid cysts)
* **Affected Organ / System:** Central Nervous System (CNS) / Meningeal spaces, ventricles, or brain parenchyma

---

## 1. Clinical Presentation & Symptoms
* **Common Presenting Symptoms:**
  * **Asymptomatic (Majority):** Most cerebral cysts are congenital, stable, and discovered incidentally on imaging performed for unrelated reasons (e.g., trauma, headaches).
  * **Symptomatic Cysts (Due to Mass Effect or CSF Obstruction):**
    * **Headache, Nausea, and Vomiting:** Caused by localized mass effect or elevated intracranial pressure.
    * **Seizures:** Occur when cortical cysts (like arachnoid or epidermoid cysts) compress the adjacent cerebral cortex.
    * **Focal Deficits & Cranial Neuropathies:** Diplopia, facial numbness, hearing loss, or hemiparesis, particularly common in cerebellopontine angle (CPA) epidermoid cysts compressing cranial nerves.
    * **Sudden Collapse or Death:** Classic for **Colloid Cysts** of the third ventricle, which can act as a ball-valve, abruptly obstructing the foramen of Monro and causing acute, fatal obstructive hydrocephalus.
    * **Developmental Delay / Focal Deficits:** In porencephalic cysts, reflecting the loss of functional brain tissue during development.

## 2. Radiographic & Imaging Findings
### Spatial & Morphological Patterns
* **Arachnoid Cyst:**
  * **Location:** Extra-axial. Most common in the middle cranial fossa (sylvian fissure, ~50–60%), followed by the cerebellopontine angle (CPA), suprasellar cistern, and quadrigeminal cistern.
  * **Morphology:** Well-demarcated, smooth, round-to-oval fluid collection. It displaces adjacent brain parenchyma and can cause remodeling or scalloping of the overlying inner table of the skull.
* **Epidermoid Cyst:**
  * **Location:** Extra-axial. Most common in the CPA (~40%), suprasellar cistern, and fourth ventricle.
  * **Morphology:** Irregular, lobulated, "pearly" mass that grows along CSF pathways, filling and expanding cisterns. Uniquely, it surrounds and engulfs adjacent cranial nerves and vessels rather than displacing them.
* **Colloid Cyst:**
  * **Location:** Anterosuperior aspect of the third ventricle, immediately posterior to the foramen of Monro.
  * **Morphology:** Smooth, round, small (typically 5 to 15 mm) well-circumscribed cyst.
* **Porencephalic Cyst:**
  * **Location:** Intra-parenchymal. 
  * **Morphology:** CSF-filled cavity lined by gliotic white matter that communicates directly with the ventricular system or the subarachnoid space.

### Signal & Density Characteristics
The signal characteristics are crucial for differentiating between these benign cystic lesions:

| Cyst Type | T1-Weighted MRI | T2-Weighted MRI | FLAIR MRI | DWI / ADC (Diffusion) | CT Density / Contrast |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Arachnoid Cyst** | Hypointense (matches CSF) | Hyperintense (matches CSF) | **Suppressed (Black)** | Hypointense / Normal (No restriction) | CSF Density (0–10 HU) / No enhancement |
| **Epidermoid Cyst** | Iso- to slightly hyperintense to CSF ("dirty CSF") | Hyperintense (matches CSF) | **Incomplete Suppression (Heterogeneous / Gray)** | **Markedly Hyperintense (Restricted / Bright DWI, Dark ADC)** | CSF Density (10–20 HU) / No enhancement |
| **Colloid Cyst** | **Markedly Hyperintense (60%)** or variable | Hypointense to variable (often dark center) | Variable | Variable | **Hyperdense (60% of cases, 40–80 HU)** / No enhancement |
| **Porencephalic Cyst** | Hypointense (matches CSF) | Hyperintense (matches CSF) | **Suppressed (Black)** with a hyperintense rim of gliosis | Hypointense / Normal (No restriction) | CSF Density / No enhancement |

## 3. Pathognomonic & Key Diagnostic Features
* **Pathognomonic Signs:**
  * **DWI Hyperintensity in a CSF-like Extra-axial Mass:** Pathognomonic for Epidermoid Cyst. This is the single most critical finding to differentiate it from an arachnoid cyst, which does not restrict diffusion.
  * **Anterosuperior Third Ventricle Location with T1 Hyperintensity:** Pathognomonic for a Colloid Cyst.
* **Key Radiologic Clues:**
  * **Engulfing of Vessels/Nerves:** Epidermoid cysts grow slowly around vessels and nerves without displacing them.
  * **Inner Table Scalloping:** Thinning and outward bowing of the skull adjacent to a middle fossa arachnoid cyst, indicating a long-standing, benign pressure effect.

## 4. Disease Progression & Temporal Evolution
* **Short-Term Progression:** 
  * Arachnoid, porencephalic, and pineal cysts are typically static and do not change in size over short intervals.
  * Colloid cysts can acutely cause ventricle dilatation if they shift position and block the foramen of Monro.
* **Long-Term Evolution / Complications:**
  * **Epidermoid Cysts:** Grow very slowly by accumulating keratinaceous debris from the lining cells. Spontaneous rupture can release irritating fatty acids, causing severe, chronic chemical meningitis.
  * **Arachnoid Cysts:** Can rarely rupture spontaneously or following minor trauma. This can lead to a subdural hematoma (due to tearing of bridging vessels) or a subdural hygroma.
  * **Porencephalic Cysts:** Represent a mature, chronic state of prior tissue destruction (e.g., perinatal stroke or trauma) and do not progress, though adjacent brain tissue may show developmental anomalies.

## 5. Management, Treatment & Follow-up Protocol
* **Standard Treatment Options:**
  * **Asymptomatic / Incidental Cysts:** Conservative "watchful waiting" is the standard. No intervention is indicated.
  * **Symptomatic Arachnoid Cysts:** 
    * Surgical fenestration (open craniotomy or endoscopic) of the cyst wall into the basal cisterns/subarachnoid space.
    * Cystoperitoneal shunting (rarely, due to shunt dependency risks).
  * **Symptomatic / Large Epidermoid Cysts:** Microsurgical resection. Complete excision of the cyst wall is preferred, but subtotal resection is acceptable to protect adherent cranial nerves or vessels.
  * **Colloid Cysts:** Surgical excision (endoscopic third ventricle resection or transcallosal craniotomy) is highly recommended even for small or minimally symptomatic cysts due to the unpredictable risk of sudden death.
* **Follow-up Imaging Guidelines:**
  * **Incidental Cysts:** A single follow-up MRI or CT at 1 year is reasonable to confirm stability. If stable, routine imaging is discontinued.
  * **Post-Operative Epidermoid:** MRI (specifically using DWI sequences) is performed every 1 to 2 years to monitor for slow recurrence from residual capsule tissue.
