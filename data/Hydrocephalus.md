# Hydrocephalus (Ventricle Enlargement / Excess CSF)

* **Full Name / Aliases:** Hydrocephalus, Active Ventriculomegaly, Obstructive (Non-communicating) Hydrocephalus, Communicating Hydrocephalus, Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH)
* **ICD-10 Code:** 
  * G91.9 (Hydrocephalus, unspecified)
  * G91.0 (Communicating hydrocephalus)
  * G91.1 (Obstructive hydrocephalus)
  * G91.2 (Normal pressure hydrocephalus / Idiopathic normal pressure hydrocephalus [iNPH])
  * Q03.9 (Congenital hydrocephalus, unspecified)
* **Affected Organ / System:** Central Nervous System (CNS) / Ventricular System and Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) circulation pathway

---

## 1. Clinical Presentation & Symptoms
* **Acute Obstructive Hydrocephalus:**
  * **Symptoms:** Sudden, severe headache, nausea, vomiting, lethargy, irritability, and altered mental status.
  * **Physical Signs:** Papilledema, abducens nerve (CN VI) palsy causing diplopia, and upward gaze palsy (Parinaud's syndrome or "setting-sun" eyes due to pressure on the tectal plate).
* **Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH) - Hakim's Triad ("Wet, Wacky, and Wobbly"):**
  * **Gait Disturbance ("Wobbly"):** Typically the first symptom. Characterized by a slow, wide-based, short-stepped, "magnetic" gait (feet appear glued to the floor), with difficulty turning.
  * **Cognitive Decline ("Wacky"):** Subcortical dementia presenting with psychomotor slowing, apathy, forgetfulness, and executive dysfunction.
  * **Urinary Incontinence ("Wet"):** Initially manifests as urinary urgency and frequency, progressing to complete loss of bladder control.
* **Congenital / Pediatric Hydrocephalus:**
  * **Symptoms:** Rapidly increasing head circumference, bulging fontanelle, prominent scalp veins, poor feeding, irritability, and downward-deviated eyes.

## 2. Radiographic & Imaging Findings
### Spatial & Morphological Patterns
* **Ventricular Dilation Pattern:**
  * **Obstructive (Non-communicating):** Dilation of the ventricles upstream of the block. For example, in **aqueductal stenosis**, the lateral and third ventricles are markedly dilated, while the fourth ventricle remains normal in size.
  * **Communicating:** Dilation of the entire ventricular system (lateral, third, and fourth ventricles) out of proportion to the subarachnoid sulci.
* **Temporal Horn Dilation:** Dilation of the temporal horns of the lateral ventricles (width >2 mm in the absence of severe hippocampal atrophy) is a highly sensitive and early sign of active hydrocephalus.
* **Frontal Horn Ballooning:** The frontal horns lose their normal sharp, angular shape and appear rounded and ballooned.
* **Callosal Angle & Displacement:** Elevation, stretching, and thinning of the corpus callosum with a callosal angle <90° (measured on coronal MRI at the level of the posterior commissure).

### Signal & Density Characteristics
* **MRI Signal Profile:**
  * **Ventricles:** Contain normal CSF signal (T1 dark, T2 bright, FLAIR suppressed/black).
  * **Transependymal CSF Flow (Interstitial Edema):** Represents CSF forced through the ependymal lining into the surrounding parenchyma due to elevated pressure.
    * **T1-weighted:** Ill-defined hypointense halos around the ventricles.
    * **T2 / FLAIR:** Smooth, symmetric, crescent-shaped hyperintensity surrounding the frontal and occipital horns.
  * **CSF Flow Void:** A marked loss of signal (black flow void) within the aqueduct of Sylvius on T2-weighted images, reflecting hyperdynamic, high-velocity CSF flow (classically prominent in NPH).
* **CT Density Profile:** 
  * Enlarged ventricles containing CSF-density fluid (0–10 HU).
  * Smooth, periventricular hypodensities around the frontal horns representing active transependymal edema.

## 3. Pathognomonic & Key Diagnostic Features
* **Pathognomonic Signs:** None strictly pathognomonic, but highly characteristic patterns exist:
  * **DESH (Disproportionately Enlarged Subarachnoid Space Hydrocephalus):** Dilated Sylvian fissures and basal cisterns with narrowing/crowding of the sulci along the high parasagittal frontoparietal convexities. This pattern is highly specific for Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH) and predicts a positive response to shunting.
* **Key Radiologic Clues:**
  * **Evans Index > 0.3:** The ratio of the maximum width of the frontal horns to the maximum internal diameter of the skull on the same slice. A value greater than 0.3 indicates pathological ventriculomegaly.
  * **Upward Bowing of the Corpus Callosum:** Seen clearly on sagittal MRI, indicating physical upward pressure from dilated lateral ventricles.

## 4. Disease Progression & Temporal Evolution
* **Short-Term Progression:** 
  * In acute obstruction, progressive ventricular expansion raises intracranial pressure rapidly, risking transtentorial or tonsillar herniation, brainstem compression, coma, and death.
* **Long-Term Evolution / Complications:**
  * In chronic or normal-pressure hydrocephalus, chronic stretching of the periventricular white matter tracts (specifically the long corticospinal tract fibers supplying the legs and bladder) leads to permanent gait impairment, irreversible subcortical dementia, and bladder detrusor instability.
  * Ventriculomegaly ex vacuo (passive expansion due to tissue loss, e.g., in Alzheimer's) must be distinguished from active hydrocephalus: ex vacuo shows proportional sulcal widening and no transependymal edema or callosal bowing.

## 5. Management, Treatment & Follow-up Protocol
* **Standard Treatment Options:**
  * **Surgical Diversion (Shunting):**
    * **Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) Shunt:** Most common treatment. A catheter is placed in the lateral ventricle, routed subcutaneously, and drained into the peritoneal cavity (often using a programmable valve to adjust CSF flow).
    * **Ventriculoatrial (VA) or Ventriculopleural Shunt:** Alternatives if peritoneal absorption is impaired.
    * **Lumboperitoneal (LP) Shunt:** Sometimes used for communicating hydrocephalus or NPH.
  * **Endoscopic Third Ventriculostomy (ETV):** An alternative to shunting for obstructive hydrocephalus (e.g., aqueductal stenosis). A fenestration is made in the floor of the third ventricle, allowing CSF to bypass the obstruction and drain directly into the prepontine cistern.
  * **NPH Selection (Tap Test):** High-volume lumbar puncture (removal of 30–50 mL of CSF). Temporary improvement in gait or cognition post-tap confirms NPH and predicts shunt success.
* **Follow-up Imaging Guidelines:**
  * **Baseline Post-op:** CT or MRI within 24–48 hours to confirm proper shunt catheter tip placement and assess for post-surgical hemorrhage.
  * **Routine Surveillance:** Imaging is performed to check for decrease in ventricular size and resolution of transependymal edema.
  * **Shunt Malfunction Protocol:** Evaluated when patient presents with recurrent symptoms. Includes a **Shunt Series** (plain radiographs of the skull, neck, chest, and abdomen to check for catheter breaks or kinks) and a rapid head CT or brain MRI to assess for acute increase in ventricular size.
