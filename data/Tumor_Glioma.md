# Tumor_Glioma (Glioma Brain Tumors)

* **Full Name / Aliases:** Glioma, Astrocytoma (IDH-mutant), Oligodendroglioma (IDH-mutant and 1p/19q-codeleted), Glioblastoma (IDH-wildtype), Diffuse Midline Glioma (H3 K27-altered), Ependymoma
* **ICD-10 Code:** 
  * C71.9 (Malignant neoplasm of brain, unspecified)
  * D33.2 (Benign neoplasm of brain, unspecified)
  * D43.2 (Neoplasm of uncertain behavior of brain, unspecified)
* **Affected Organ / System:** Central Nervous System (CNS) / Brain and Spinal Cord (glial support cells: astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, ependymal cells)

---

## 1. Clinical Presentation & Symptoms
* **Common Presenting Symptoms:** 
  * **Seizures:** New-onset seizures are a classic presentation, especially for lower-grade (CNS WHO grade 2) gliomas in young adults.
  * **Headaches:** Often worse in the morning, aggravated by coughing, bending, or physical exertion, and associated with nausea/vomiting due to increased intracranial pressure (ICP).
  * **Focal Neurological Deficits:** Progressive motor weakness, sensory changes, speech disturbances (aphasia), or visual field cuts depending on the anatomical site.
  * **Cognitive & Behavioral Changes:** Memory loss, confusion, personality changes, apathy, and executive dysfunction.
* **Clinical Course:** Progressive. Low-grade gliomas (CNS WHO grade 2) present with a slow, indolent course over years, whereas high-grade gliomas (Glioblastoma, IDH-wildtype, or Astrocytoma, IDH-mutant, CNS WHO grade 4) progress rapidly over weeks or months.

## 2. Radiographic & Imaging Findings
### Spatial & Morphological Patterns
* **Location:** Most commonly located in the cerebral hemispheres (lobar: frontal, temporal, parietal). Diffuse midline gliomas occur in the brainstem (e.g., pons - diffuse intrinsic pontine glioma [DIPG]), thalamus, or spinal cord. Ependymomas are often intraventricular (fourth ventricle) or spinal.
* **Size & Shape:** Highly variable. Lower-grade gliomas tend to be homogeneous, non-enhancing, and ill-defined. High-grade gliomas (such as Glioblastoma, IDH-wildtype) are large, heterogeneous, infiltrative masses with irregular margins, significant mass effect, and midline shift.
* **Infiltration Pattern:** Infiltrate along white matter tracts. Glioblastoma classically crosses the midline via the corpus callosum.

### Signal & Density Characteristics
* **MRI Signal Profile:**
  * **T1-weighted:** Hypointense relative to normal gray/white matter.
  * **T2-weighted / FLAIR:** Hyperintense. High-grade gliomas demonstrate extensive surrounding FLAIR hyperintensity, representing a mixture of vasogenic edema and infiltrating microscopic tumor cells.
  * **DWI / ADC:** 
    * *Grade 2:* Elevated ADC (bright on ADC, dark on DWI) reflecting low cellularity and absence of tissue density.
    * *Grade 3-4 (High-grade):* Restricted diffusion (dark on ADC, bright on DWI) within the solid, hypercellular components of the tumor.
  * **T1 + Contrast (T1+C):** 
    * *Low-grade (CNS WHO grade 2):* Typically shows no enhancement or only faint, patchy enhancement.
    * *High-grade (CNS WHO grade 3-4 / Glioblastoma):* Thick, irregular, nodular, or ring-like enhancement surrounding a non-enhancing central necrotic core. Note: Glioblastoma, IDH-wildtype can be diagnosed molecularly (e.g., TERT mutation, EGFR amplification, +7/-10) even in the absence of classic histologic/imaging features like necrosis or ring-enhancement.
* **CT Density Profile:** 
  * Low-grade gliomas are generally hypodense or isodense, occasionally with calcifications (especially oligodendrogliomas).
  * High-grade gliomas appear as complex, mixed-density masses (hypodense necrosis/cysts, isodense/hyperdense solid components, and hyperdense acute intratumoral hemorrhage) with marked surrounding hypodense vasogenic edema.
* **MR Spectroscopy (MRS) Findings:**
  * **Choline (Cho) Peak:** Elevated (indicates cell membrane synthesis and high turnover).
  * **N-acetylaspartate (NAA) Peak:** Decreased (indicates loss of healthy neuronal tissue).
  * **Lactate / Lipid Peak:** Elevated at 1.3 ppm (indicates anaerobic metabolism and tissue necrosis in high-grade gliomas).
  * **2-Hydroxyglutarate (2-HG) Peak:** Elevated at 2.25 ppm (specific biomarker for IDH-mutant gliomas, detectable on specialized MR spectroscopy).

## 3. Pathognomonic & Key Diagnostic Features
* **Pathognomonic Signs:**
  * **"Butterfly Glioma":** A large, bi-hemispheric frontal lobe mass crossing the corpus callosum with a central necrotic cavity, highly specific for Glioblastoma, IDH-wildtype (or CNS lymphoma).
* **Key Radiologic Clues:**
  * **T2-FLAIR Mismatch Sign:** A highly specific indicator for **Astrocytoma, IDH-mutant** (CNS WHO grade 2 or 3). The lesion shows homogeneous T2 hyperintensity but complete or near-complete suppression (hypointensity) on FLAIR, except for a hyperintense peripheral rim.
  * **Cortical Scalloping:** Oligodendrogliomas are often cortical-subcortical, slow-growing, and display calcification in up to 90% of cases, often causing remodeling/scalloping of the overlying inner table of the skull.
  * **Cyst with an Enhancing Mural Nodule:** Classic for Pilocytic Astrocytoma (CNS WHO grade 1), typically in the cerebellum of pediatric patients.

## 4. Disease Progression & Temporal Evolution
* **Short-Term Progression:** Rapid expansion in high-grade lesions, leading to mass effect, compression of the ventricles (obstructive hydrocephalus), midline shift, and herniation syndromes (subfalcine, transtentorial, or tonsillar herniation).
* **Long-Term Evolution / Malignant Transformation:**
  * Lower-grade IDH-mutant astrocytomas (CNS WHO grade 2) inevitably undergo malignant transformation/de-differentiation over time into higher grades, culminating in **Astrocytoma, IDH-mutant, CNS WHO grade 4** (previously referred to as secondary glioblastoma; the term "Glioblastoma" is now reserved exclusively for IDH-wildtype tumors).
  * Molecular progression is marked by the acquisition of homozygous deletion of **CDKN2A/B** (which automatically upgrades an IDH-mutant astrocytoma to grade 4) or ATRX loss.
  * Late-stage features include widespread necrosis, increased tumor vascularity, remote metastases along CSF pathways (leptomeningeal seeding), and intratumoral hemorrhage.

## 5. Management, Treatment & Follow-up Protocol
* **Standard Treatment Options:**
  * **Surgical Resection:** Maximal safe resection to reduce mass effect and obtain tissue for integrated histological-molecular diagnosis (IDH1/2 mutation status, 1p/19q co-deletion, MGMT promoter methylation, CDKN2A/B status, and ATRX expression).
  * **Adjuvant Therapy:** Radiotherapy and chemotherapy (typically Temozolomide [Stupp protocol] for Glioblastoma, IDH-wildtype and high-grade astrocytomas; PCV regimen [Procarbazine, Lomustine, Vincristine] for Oligodendrogliomas).
  * **Symptomatic Therapy:** Corticosteroids (e.g., Dexamethasone) for edema control; anti-epileptic drugs (AEDs) for seizure management.
* **Follow-up Imaging Guidelines:**
  * **Early Post-op:** MRI within 24–48 hours to establish a baseline and assess the extent of resection.
  * **High-grade (CNS WHO grade 3–4):** Brain MRI with and without contrast every 2 to 4 months.
  * **Low-grade (CNS WHO grade 2):** Brain MRI every 6 to 12 months, or sooner if clinical status changes.
  * **Special Consideration (Pseudoprogression):** Transient increase in contrast enhancement and edema occurring within 3-6 months post-radiotherapy, mimicry of tumor recurrence. Advanced techniques like MR Perfusion (increased rCBV in recurrence, low/normal in pseudoprogression) or PET-CT are used to differentiate.
