# Hemorrhage (Intracranial Bleeding)

* **Full Name / Aliases:** Intracranial Hemorrhage (ICH), Intracranial Bleeding, Brain Bleed, Epidural Hematoma (EDH), Subdural Hematoma (SDH), Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH), Intraparenchymal (Intracerebral) Hemorrhage (IPH), Intraventricular Hemorrhage (IVH)
* **ICD-10 Code:** 
  * I61.9 (Nontraumatic intracerebral hemorrhage, unspecified)
  * I60.9 (Nontraumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage, unspecified)
  * I62.00 (Nontraumatic subdural hemorrhage, unspecified)
  * S06.4X9A (Epidural hemorrhage, traumatic, initial encounter)
* **Affected Organ / System:** Central Nervous System (CNS) / Brain parenchyma, ventricles, meningeal spaces (epidural, subdural, subarachnoid), and Cerebrovascular System

---

## 1. Clinical Presentation & Symptoms
* **Common Presenting Symptoms:**
  * **Severe Headache:** Sudden, severe headache. In SAH, this is classically described as a "thunderclap headache" ("worst headache of my life").
  * **Altered Consciousness:** Confusion, lethargy, obtundation, or coma.
  * **Nausea & Vomiting:** Common due to acute elevation of intracranial pressure (ICP).
  * **Focal Neurological Deficits:** Sudden hemiplegia, hemiparesis, sensory loss, cranial nerve palsies, or speech disturbances (aphasia).
  * **Meningismus:** Neck stiffness (nuchal rigidity), photophobia, and phonophobia (classic in SAH).
  * **Seizures:** Common at onset due to cortical irritation by blood products.
  * **Lucid Interval:** Classically seen in traumatic EDH (brief loss of consciousness at impact, followed by a temporary return to normal consciousness, before rapid deterioration as the hematoma expands).
* **Clinical Course:** Hyperacute onset. Immediate medical emergency.

## 2. Radiographic & Imaging Findings
### Spatial & Morphological Patterns
* **Epidural Hematoma (EDH):**
  * **Pattern:** Biconvex, lens-shaped (lentiform) extra-axial collection.
  * **Anatomy:** Located between the inner table of the skull and the dura mater. Does **not** cross cranial sutures (dura is firmly attached there) but can cross the dural duplications (falx/tentorium). Typically associated with a skull fracture and rupture of the middle meningeal artery.
* **Subdural Hematoma (SDH):**
  * **Pattern:** Crescent-shaped (concave) extra-axial collection.
  * **Anatomy:** Located between the dura mater and the arachnoid membrane. **Crosses** cranial sutures but is limited by dural attachments (does not cross the falx cerebri or tentorium). Usually caused by tearing of bridging cortical veins.
* **Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH):**
  * **Pattern:** High attenuation (blood) tracking within the CSF-filled sulci, sylvian fissures, and basal cisterns. Classically forms a "star-like" pattern in the suprasellar cistern when caused by a ruptured aneurysm.
* **Intraparenchymal Hemorrhage (IPH / ICH):**
  * **Pattern:** Focal round or irregular collection of blood within the brain tissue.
  * **Anatomy:** Deep structures (basal ganglia, thalamus, pons, cerebellum) are typical for hypertensive bleeds. Peripheral lobar regions are typical for Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA) or trauma.
* **Intraventricular Hemorrhage (IVH):**
  * **Pattern:** High attenuation material layering in the occipital horns of the lateral ventricles or casting the entire ventricular system.

### Signal & Density Characteristics
* **CT Density Profile (First-line Screening):**
  * **Hyperacute / Acute (<72 hours):** Markedly hyperdense (bright white, 60–80 HU).
  * **Subacute (3 to 14 days):** Attenuation gradually decreases, becoming isodense (gray, 30–40 HU) relative to normal brain parenchyma.
  * **Chronic (>14 days):** Hypodense (dark, 0–20 HU), eventually reaching CSF density.
* **MRI Signal Profile (Complex Temporal Evolution):**
  The appearance of hemorrhage on MRI depends on the biochemical state of hemoglobin (oxy-, deoxy-, or methemoglobin) and whether it is intracellular or extracellular.

| Stage | Timeframe | Hemoglobin State | T1-Weighted | T2-Weighted | SWI / T2* (Susceptibility) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hyperacute** | < 24 hours | Intracellular Oxyhemoglobin | Iso- to Hypointense | Hyperintense | Isointense (mild blooming) |
| **Acute** | 1–3 days | Intracellular Deoxyhemoglobin | Iso- to Hypointense | **Markedly Hypointense** | **Marked Blooming (Black)** |
| **Early Subacute** | 3–7 days | Intracellular Methemoglobin | **Hyperintense (Bright)** | Hypointense | Marked Blooming (Black) |
| **Late Subacute** | 7–14 days | Extracellular Methemoglobin | **Hyperintense (Bright)** | **Hyperintense (Bright)** | Marked Blooming (Black) |
| **Chronic** | > 14 days | Extracellular Hemosiderin | Hypointense | **Markedly Hypointense** | **Extreme Blooming (Black Rim)** |

## 3. Pathognomonic & Key Diagnostic Features
* **Pathognomonic Signs:**
  * **Biconvex / Lentiform collection:** Pathognomonic for Epidural Hematoma (EDH).
  * **Crescentic collection:** Pathognomonic for Subdural Hematoma (SDH).
  * **Star-shaped suprasellar hyperdensity:** Pathognomonic for Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH) secondary to ruptured circle of Willis aneurysm.
* **Key Radiologic Clues:**
  * **CTA "Spot Sign":** One or more focal areas of contrast extravasation within an acute intraparenchymal hematoma on CT Angiography. Highly predictive of hematoma expansion and poor prognosis.
  * **SWI Blooming:** Severe signal loss (blackout) on susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) due to iron contents, extremely sensitive for detecting tiny petechial hemorrhages or chronic microbleeds.

## 4. Disease Progression & Temporal Evolution
* **Short-Term Progression:** 
  * **Hematoma Expansion:** Up to 30% of IPHs expand within the first few hours, leading to acute mass effect, ventricular compression, midline shift, and herniation.
  * **Rebleed Risk:** High in aneurysmal SAH (greatest in the first 24 hours).
  * **Vasospasm & Delayed Cerebral Ischemia (DCI):** Classic complication of SAH, occurring 3 to 14 days post-bleed (peak at 7-10 days), where chemical breakdown of subarachnoid blood causes arterial constriction and secondary strokes.
* **Long-Term Evolution / Complications:**
  * Hydrocephalus (communicating or obstructive) due to blood clogging the arachnoid granulations (especially in SAH/IVH).
  * Encephalomalacia: Resorption of intraparenchymal blood leaves a cystic cavity surrounded by a rim of hemosiderin-stained, gliotic tissue.

## 5. Management, Treatment & Follow-up Protocol
* **Standard Treatment Options:**
  * **Emergency Medical Stabilization:**
    * Immediate airway control (if GCS < 8).
    * Rapid, aggressive blood pressure control (e.g., target systolic BP <140 mmHg for acute spontaneous ICH).
    * Reversal of coagulopathies: Vitamin K and Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (PCC) for Warfarin; Idarucizumab for Dabigatran; Andexanet Alfa for Apixaban/Rivaroxaban.
  * **ICP Management:** Elevation of the head, hypertonic saline (3%), Mannitol, or temporary hyperventilation.
  * **Surgical Intervention:**
    * **Evacuation (Craniotomy):** Indicated for EDH >30 mL, acute SDH >10 mm thickness or causing >5 mm midline shift, or cerebellar hemorrhage >3 cm causing brainstem compression.
    * **Aneurysm Securing:** Surgical clipping or endovascular coiling within 24 hours for aneurysmal SAH.
    * **External Ventricular Drain (EVD):** Placed in the lateral ventricle to drain blood and CSF to control ICP in cases of IVH or acute hydrocephalus.
* **Follow-up Imaging Guidelines:**
  * **Serial Non-Contrast CT:** Performed at 6 hours post-admission (or immediately if clinical status worsens) to evaluate for hematoma expansion.
  * **Vascular Workup:** CT Angiography (CTA), MR Angiography (MRA), or Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA - gold standard) to rule out aneurysms, arteriovenous malformations (AVMs), or dural arteriovenous fistulas (dAVFs).
  * **Long-Term Follow-up:** Brain MRI at 6-12 weeks to evaluate for underlying lesions (such as cavernomas, tumors, or cerebral amyloid angiopathy) that might have been obscured by the acute hemorrhage.
