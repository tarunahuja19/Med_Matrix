# AVM (Arteriovenous Malformation)

* **Full Name / Aliases:** Brain Arteriovenous Malformation, Cerebral AVM, Vascular Malformation
* **ICD-10 Code:** Q28.2 (Arteriovenous malformation of cerebral vessels - congenital vascular anomaly)
* **Affected Organ / System:** Central Nervous System (CNS) / Cerebrovascular System (Arterial and Venous vessels)

---

## 1. Clinical Presentation & Symptoms
* **Common Presenting Symptoms:**
  * **Intracranial Hemorrhage (40–50% of cases):** Most common and serious presentation. Can lead to intraparenchymal, subarachnoid, or intraventricular hemorrhage, causing sudden, severe headache, focal neurological deficits, or coma.
  * **Seizures (20–30% of cases):** Generalized or focal seizures due to cortical irritation from blood products or chronic localized ischemia.
  * **Headache (10–15% of cases):** Chronic, recurrent, sometimes migraine-like or throbbing headaches, often caused by increased meningeal blood flow or dural distension.
  * **Focal Neurological Deficits:** Progressive weakness, sensory loss, visual field defects, or language impairment. This can occur in the absence of hemorrhage due to:
    * **Mass Effect:** Compression of adjacent brain tissue by dilated vessels.
    * **Steal Phenomenon:** Hypoperfusion of normal brain tissue due to blood shunting rapidly through the low-resistance AVM.
  * **Pulsatile Tinnitus:** A whooshing sound in the ear synchronized with the heartbeat, particularly common in AVMs near the dural sinuses or skull base.
* **Clinical Course:** Congenital. Usually remains clinically silent until presenting in young adulthood (typical age of diagnosis is 20–40 years).

## 2. Radiographic & Imaging Findings
### Spatial & Morphological Patterns
* **Anatomy of an AVM:** Composed of three distinct parts:
  1. **Feeding Arteries:** Dilated, high-flow arteries (typically branches of the MCA, ACA, or PCA).
  2. **Nidus (The Core):** A tangled mass of dysplastic, interconnected vascular channels lacking an intervening capillary bed.
  3. **Draining Veins:** Markedly dilated, tortuous veins showing early contrast opacification (arterialized veins).
* **Location:** 85% are supratentorial (lobar cerebral hemispheres, deep basal ganglia, or thalamus). 15% are infratentorial (cerebellum or brainstem).
* **Size:** Highly variable, ranging from micro-AVMs (<1 cm) to giant malformations (>6 cm).

### Signal & Density Characteristics
* **MRI Signal Profile (Excellent for anatomical delineation):**
  * **T1- and T2-weighted:** The hallmark finding is **multiple serpentine flow voids** (areas of signal loss/black tubes) representing high-velocity blood flow through the nidus and draining veins.
  * **FLAIR:** The surrounding brain tissue may display hyperintensity, representing gliosis, chronic ischemia, or vasogenic edema from venous congestion.
  * **MRA / CTA:** Demonstrates rapid filling of feeding arteries, the structural tangle of the nidus, and early visualization of draining veins.
  * **SWI / T2* (Susceptibility):** Demonstrates areas of blooming (black signal loss) from chronic hemosiderin staining (due to prior microbleeds) or intralesional calcification.
* **CT Density Profile:**
  * **Non-contrast CT:** Appears as an irregular, slightly hyperdense or isodense mass. Calcification is present in up to 30% of cases. There is typically no surrounding edema or mass effect unless acute rupture has occurred.
  * **Contrast CT (CTA):** Prominent enhancement of the nidus, feeding arteries, and early draining veins.
* **Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA - Gold Standard):**
  * Directly evaluates dynamic flow. Pathognomonic finding is **early venous filling** (appearance of veins in the arterial phase of the injection), indicating a high-flow shunt bypassing the capillary bed.

## 3. Pathognomonic & Key Diagnostic Features
* **Pathognomonic Signs:**
  * **"Bag of Worms" Appearance:** A collection of serpentine, tubular flow voids on T1 and T2-weighted MRI.
  * **Early Venous Filling on Angiography:** Draining veins opacifying during the early arterial phase of contrast injection due to the absence of capillaries.
* **Key Radiologic Clues:**
  * **Spetzler-Martin Grading System:** A widely used classification system (Grades I to V) to estimate the morbidity and mortality risk of surgical resection. The grade is the sum of three scores:

| Feature | Category | Score |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Size of Nidus** | Small (< 3 cm) <br> Medium (3–6 cm) <br> Large (> 6 cm) | 1 <br> 2 <br> 3 |
| **Eloquence of Brain Tissue** | Non-eloquent <br> Eloquent (motor/sensory/speech/visual cortex, brainstem, thalamus) | 0 <br> 1 |
| **Venous Drainage Pattern** | Superficial veins only <br> Deep draining veins | 0 <br> 1 |

## 4. Disease Progression & Temporal Evolution
* **Short-Term Progression:** In the event of acute rupture, hematoma formation behaves like a standard intraparenchymal or subarachnoid hemorrhage, risking mass effect, herniation, and elevated ICP.
* **Long-Term Evolution / Complications:**
  * **Hemorrhage Risk:** Unruptured AVMs carry a cumulative annual rupture risk of approximately **2% to 4%**. Following a rupture, the risk of rebleeding rises to **15% to 20%** within the first year.
  * **Aneurysm Formation:** Flow-related aneurysms can develop in the feeding arteries (due to high hemodynamic stress) or within the nidus itself, further increasing the risk of rupture.
  * Progressive gliosis and tissue atrophy in the surrounding brain due to chronic venous hypertension and arterial "steal."

## 5. Management, Treatment & Follow-up Protocol
* **Standard Treatment Options:**
  * **Surgical Resection (Microsurgery):** Direct craniotomy and dissection of the nidus. This is the definitive cure, highly successful for lower-grade AVMs (Spetzler-Martin Grades I–III).
  * **Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS):** Gamma Knife or Linac radiosurgery. High-dose radiation causes slow endothelial proliferation, vessel wall thickening, and eventual thrombosis/obliteration of the nidus over 1 to 3 years. Best for small (<3 cm) AVMs located in deep, surgically inaccessible, or highly eloquent areas (e.g., thalamus, brainstem).
  * **Endovascular Embolization:** Catheterization of feeding vessels and injection of liquid embolic agents (e.g., Onyx, Cyanoacrylate glue) to occlude high-flow channels. Usually performed as an adjunct prior to surgery or radiosurgery to reduce nidus size and blood flow.
  * **Conservative Medical Management:** Anti-epileptic drugs for seizures, blood pressure control, and pain management. Indicated for high-grade (Grade IV–V) unruptured AVMs, where the risk of intervention exceeds the natural history risk of bleeding (supported by the ARUBA trial).
* **Follow-up Imaging Guidelines:**
  * **Post-Treatment Confirmation:** A follow-up Digital Subtraction Angiogram (DSA) is required to confirm complete obliteration.
  * **Post-Radiosurgery Surveillance:** Brain MRI/MRA every 6 to 12 months for 3 years to monitor the rate of nidus shrinkage, radiation-induced edema, and to check for final obliteration (confirmed by DSA).
  * **Long-Term Surveillance:** For unruptured AVMs managed conservatively, annual MRI/MRA is recommended to monitor for changes in venous drainage or feeding artery aneurysm formation.
